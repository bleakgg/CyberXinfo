Common Attack Targets

Cyber attackers don't strike at random—they have specific goals in mind and target systems, data, and individuals that offer the highest value or lowest resistance.

Overview

Attack targets represent the valuable assets that cybercriminals aim to compromise. These can include personal information, financial data, intellectual property, system access, and computational resources. Understanding what attackers want helps us better protect our most valuable digital assets.

Personal Data

This includes personally identifiable information (PII) such as full names, addresses, Social Security numbers, and dates of birth. This information can be used for identity theft or sold on dark web marketplaces.

Financial Information

Credit card numbers, banking credentials, investment accounts, and cryptocurrency wallets are prime targets as they provide direct monetary gain for attackers.

Login Credentials

Usernames and passwords for various services can provide access to valuable accounts and are often used in credential stuffing attacks across multiple platforms.

Corporate Systems

Business networks, databases, and infrastructure are targeted for sensitive corporate data, intellectual property, or as launching points for further attacks.

How They Are Targeted

Attackers use various techniques to reach their targets:

• Reconnaissance - Gathering information about potential targets
• Vulnerability scanning - Identifying weaknesses in systems
• Social engineering - Manipulating humans to gain access
• Supply chain attacks - Compromising trusted vendors or software
• Removable media - Infected USB drives or external hard drives
• Brute force attacks - Repeatedly trying different passwords

How to Protect Your Assets

  • Data minimization - Only share necessary information with services and platforms
  • Asset inventory - Know what sensitive data you have and where it's stored
  • Access controls - Implement the principle of least privilege for all accounts
  • Regular backups - Maintain offline copies of critical data
  • Security monitoring - Use tools to detect unusual access or data movements
  • Encryption - Protect sensitive data both in transit and at rest
